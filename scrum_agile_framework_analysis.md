This document provides a comprehensive analysis of why the Scrum-Agile framework is the optimal choice for our educational platform development project, considering our team composition (1 Product Owner/Mentor, 1 Scrum Master, 1 Tech Lead, and 3 Developers) and project timeline (14 weeks).

## 2. What is Scrum-Agile Framework

### 2.1 Definition
Scrum is a lightweight Agile framework that helps teams work together to develop, deliver, and sustain complex products through iterative and incremental approaches. It is specifically designed to deliver maximum value in minimum time.

### 2.2 Key Characteristics
- **Iterative Development**: Work is divided into short cycles called Sprints (typically 1-4 weeks)
- **Incremental Delivery**: Each Sprint produces a potentially shippable product increment
- **Empirical Process Control**: Based on transparency, inspection, and adaptation
- **Self-Organizing Teams**: Teams decide how to accomplish their work
- **Time-Boxed Events**: Fixed-duration events to create regularity and minimize waste

## 3. Core Principles and Values

### 3.1 Agile Manifesto Values
1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan

### 3.2 Scrum Values
- **Commitment**: Team members commit to achieving team goals
- **Courage**: Team members have courage to do the right thing and work on tough problems
- **Focus**: Everyone focuses on the Sprint work and team goals
- **Openness**: Team and stakeholders are open about work and challenges
- **Respect**: Team members respect each other as capable, independent people

### 3.3 Agile Principles (Relevant to Small Team)
- Deliver working software frequently (weeks rather than months)
- Welcome changing requirements, even late in development
- Business people and developers work together daily
- Build projects around motivated individuals
- Face-to-face conversation is the most efficient method of conveying information
- Working software is the primary measure of progress
- Sustainable development pace
- Continuous attention to technical excellence
- Simplicity—the art of maximizing work not done
- Self-organizing teams produce the best architectures and designs
- Regular reflection on how to become more effective

## 4. Scrum Framework Components

### 4.1 Scrum Roles

#### Product Owner (PO)
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Manages and prioritizes Product Backlog
  - Defines acceptance criteria
  - Represents stakeholders and end-users
  - Maximizes value of product and team's work
  - Makes decisions about product direction
- **In Our Context**: Lê Mai Đông (Mentor/Supervisor)

#### Scrum Master (SM)
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Facilitates Scrum events
  - Removes impediments blocking team progress
  - Coaches team on Scrum practices
  - Protects team from external interruptions
  - Promotes self-organization
  - Tracks team velocity and metrics
- **In Our Context**: Mai Tường Huy

#### Development Team
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Self-organizing and cross-functional
  - Delivers potentially shippable increments each Sprint
  - Collectively responsible for Sprint success
  - Estimates work and commits to Sprint Goals
  - Performs all development activities (design, code, test, documentation)
- **In Our Context**: 
  - Tech Lead: Phùng Minh Nghĩa
  - Developers: Nguyễn Cảnh Dương, Đặng Văn Toàn, Nguyễn Hữu Hoàng Anh

### 4.2 Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

#### Sprint (1-4 weeks)
- Container for all other events
- Fixed-length iteration
- Creates predictability
- **Our Choice**: 2-week Sprints (7 Sprints in 14 weeks)

#### Sprint Planning (4-8 hours for 2-week Sprint)
- Team selects items from Product Backlog
- Creates Sprint Goal
- Plans how to deliver the increment
- **Our Context**: ~2-3 hours (scaled for small team)

#### Daily Scrum (15 minutes)
- Daily synchronization meeting
- Team members share:
  - What did I do yesterday?
  - What will I do today?
  - Are there any impediments?
- **Our Context**: 15 minutes, same time daily

#### Sprint Review (2-4 hours for 2-week Sprint)
- Team demonstrates completed work
- Product Owner accepts/rejects work
- Stakeholders provide feedback
- **Our Context**: ~1-2 hours

#### Sprint Retrospective (1.5-3 hours for 2-week Sprint)
- Team reflects on process
- Identifies improvements
- Creates action plan for next Sprint
- **Our Context**: ~1 hour

#### Backlog Refinement (Ongoing)
- Review and update Product Backlog items
- Add details, estimates, and order
- Usually 10% of Sprint capacity
- **Our Context**: Mid-Sprint session (~1-2 hours)

### 4.3 Scrum Artifacts

#### Product Backlog
- Ordered list of everything needed in the product
- Single source of requirements
- Continuously refined and prioritized
- Items have: Description, Order, Estimate, Value

#### Sprint Backlog
- Set of Product Backlog items selected for Sprint
- Plus plan for delivering them
- Owned by Development Team
- Updated daily

#### Increment
- Sum of all completed Product Backlog items in current and all previous Sprints
- Must be in usable condition
- Must meet Definition of Done

#### Definition of Done (DoD)
- Shared understanding of what "complete" means
- Ensures transparency and quality
- Examples for our project:
  - Code written and peer-reviewed
  - Unit tests written and passing
  - Integration tests passing
  - Documentation updated
  - Code merged to main branch
  - Accepted by Product Owner

## 5. Why Choose Scrum-Agile Over Other Frameworks

### 5.1 Comparison with Waterfall

| Aspect | Waterfall | Scrum-Agile | Winner for Our Project |
|--------|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Flexibility** | Rigid, sequential phases | Highly flexible, iterative | ✓ Scrum |
| **Change Management** | Difficult and costly to change | Welcomes change at any time | ✓ Scrum |
| **Delivery Timeline** | Everything delivered at end | Working software every 2 weeks | ✓ Scrum |
| **Risk Management** | High risk discovered late | Risks identified and mitigated early | ✓ Scrum |
| **Stakeholder Feedback** | Only at beginning and end | Continuous throughout project | ✓ Scrum |
| **Requirements Clarity** | Requires complete upfront requirements | Can start with unclear requirements | ✓ Scrum |
| **Team Adaptation** | No mid-project adjustments | Regular retrospectives for improvement | ✓ Scrum |
| **Documentation** | Heavy documentation focus | Just enough documentation | ✓ Scrum |
| **Small Team Fit** | Overhead too heavy for 5 people | Designed for small teams | ✓ Scrum |
| **14-Week Timeline** | Cannot complete full cycle | Allows 7 complete iterations | ✓ Scrum |

**Waterfall Problems for Our Context**:
- 14 weeks not enough for full waterfall cycle (Requirements → Design → Development → Testing → Deployment)
- No working product until week 13-14, high risk of failure
- Cannot adapt to changing requirements from academic supervisor
- Testing only at end means bugs discovered too late
- No intermediate feedback to validate direction

### 5.2 Comparison with Kanban

| Aspect | Kanban | Scrum-Agile | Winner for Our Project |
|--------|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Structure** | Continuous flow | Time-boxed Sprints | ✓ Scrum |
| **Roles** | No prescribed roles | Clear roles (PO, SM, Dev Team) | ✓ Scrum |
| **Ceremonies** | No mandatory meetings | Structured events | ✓ Scrum |
| **Planning** | Just-in-time | Sprint Planning every 2 weeks | ✓ Scrum |
| **Commitment** | No Sprint commitment | Team commits to Sprint Goal | ✓ Scrum |
| **Change** | Change anytime | No changes during Sprint | ✓ Scrum |
| **Progress Tracking** | Lead time, cycle time | Velocity, burndown | ✓ Scrum |
| **Team Experience** | Better for mature teams | Built-in learning structure | ✓ Scrum |
| **Academic Requirements** | Less formal documentation | Sprint artifacts for reporting | ✓ Scrum |

**Why Not Kanban**:
- Too flexible for inexperienced team
- No natural checkpoints for academic reporting
- Difficult to measure progress in fixed 14-week timeline
- Lacks ceremony structure helpful for learning
- No built-in retrospective for continuous improvement
- Harder to demonstrate incremental progress to supervisor

### 5.3 Comparison with Extreme Programming (XP)

| Aspect | XP | Scrum-Agile | Winner for Our Project |
|--------|-----|-------------|------------------------|
| **Technical Practices** | Very prescriptive (TDD, pair programming) | Flexible technical practices | ✓ Scrum |
| **Release Cycle** | Very short (days) | 2-week Sprints | ✓ Scrum |
| **Customer Involvement** | On-site customer required | Regular reviews sufficient | ✓ Scrum |
| **Team Size** | Best for 2-10 developers | Optimal for 3-9 members | Both good |
| **Learning Curve** | Steep (many practices to learn) | Moderate (focus on process) | ✓ Scrum |
| **Pair Programming** | Mandatory | Optional | ✓ Scrum |
| **Planning Horizon** | Very short term | 2-week planning | ✓ Scrum |

**Why Not XP**:
- Too many technical practices to learn simultaneously
- Pair programming reduces individual productivity initially
- On-site customer not feasible (mentor has other duties)
- Too frequent releases disrupt academic schedule
- Steeper learning curve for team new to Agile

### 5.4 Comparison with Feature-Driven Development (FDD)

| Aspect | FDD | Scrum-Agile | Winner for Our Project |
|--------|-----|-------------|------------------------|
| **Complexity** | Better for large projects | Scales down well | ✓ Scrum |
| **Team Size** | Designed for 10+ developers | Perfect for 3-9 members | ✓ Scrum |
| **Documentation** | More formal modeling | Lightweight | ✓ Scrum |
| **Roles** | Many specialized roles | 3 simple roles | ✓ Scrum |
| **Popularity** | Less common | Industry standard | ✓ Scrum |
| **Learning Resources** | Limited | Abundant | ✓ Scrum |

**Why Not FDD**:
- Over-engineered for 5-person team
- Requires more upfront modeling time
- Less flexible to changes
- Harder to find learning resources
- Not well-suited for small educational projects

## 6. Suitability for Small Teams (5 Members)

### 6.1 Optimal Team Size
- Scrum recommends 3-9 Development Team members
- Our team: 1 PO + 1 SM + 1 Tech Lead + 3 Devs = 5 total
- **Perfect fit**: Large enough for diverse skills, small enough for tight communication

### 6.2 Advantages for Small Teams

#### Communication Efficiency
- **Direct Communication**: 5 people can communicate face-to-face easily
- **Minimal Overhead**: No need for complex communication structures
- **Fast Decision Making**: Small group reaches consensus quickly
- **No Information Silos**: Everyone knows what everyone else is doing
- **Daily Scrum**: 15 minutes sufficient for 5 people (3 min per dev)

#### Coordination Benefits
- **Reduced Coordination Complexity**: 
  - 5-person team = 10 communication channels
  - 10-person team = 45 communication channels
- **Self-Organization**: Easier with fewer people
- **Conflict Resolution**: Faster and simpler
- **Trust Building**: Stronger bonds in small team

#### Ceremony Efficiency

| Event | Standard Time | Our Team Time | Time Saved |
|-------|---------------|---------------|------------|
| Sprint Planning | 8 hours | 2-3 hours | 5-6 hours |
| Daily Scrum | 15 min | 15 min | No change |
| Sprint Review | 4 hours | 1-2 hours | 2-3 hours |
| Retrospective | 3 hours | 1 hour | 2 hours |
| **Total per Sprint** | ~23 hours | ~8-10 hours | **~13-15 hours saved** |

#### Flexibility and Agility
- **Quick Pivots**: Small team adapts faster to change
- **Skill Sharing**: Cross-training easier with fewer people
- **Collective Ownership**: Everyone can understand entire codebase
- **Shared Responsibility**: No role silos

### 6.3 Small Team Best Practices in Scrum

#### 1. Cross-Functional Skills
- Each developer should have multiple skills (frontend, backend, testing)
- Reduces bottlenecks and dependencies
- Our team composition supports this:
  - All developers handle both frontend and backend
  - Tech Lead provides technical guidance across all areas

#### 2. Shared Ownership
- No code ownership by individuals
- Any developer can work on any part
- Reduces "single point of failure" risk
- Increases knowledge distribution

#### 3. Simplified Tools
- Lightweight project tracking (Jira, Trello, or GitHub Projects)
- No need for complex enterprise tools
- Physical board if co-located (sticky notes)

#### 4. Focused Communication
- Daily Scrum keeps everyone aligned
- No need for status reports or lengthy meetings
- Slack/Teams for async communication
- Quick huddles when needed

#### 5. Lean Documentation
- Just enough documentation
- Living documents (updated as needed)
- Focus on working software
- User stories over lengthy requirements

## 7. Suitability for 14-Week Development Cycle

### 7.1 Sprint Planning for 14 Weeks

#### Sprint Duration: 2 Weeks
**Rationale**:
- Long enough to complete meaningful work
- Short enough for quick feedback loops
- Industry standard for small teams
- Perfect balance of planning and execution

#### Sprint Breakdown
```
14 weeks ÷ 2 weeks per Sprint = 7 Sprints

Sprint 0 (Week 1): Setup & Planning
├── Environment setup
├── Tool configuration
├── Initial backlog creation
└── Team training

Sprint 1 (Weeks 2-3): Core Foundation
├── Authentication system
├── User profile management
└── Basic UI framework

Sprint 2 (Weeks 4-5): Class Management
├── Create/Update/Delete classes
├── Class listing and search
└── Basic class features

Sprint 3 (Weeks 6-7): Student Features
├── Join class functionality
├── Class materials viewing
└── Schedule viewing

Sprint 4 (Weeks 8-9): Assignment System
├── Create assignments
├── Submit assignments
└── Basic grading

Sprint 5 (Weeks 10-11): Advanced Features
├── Resource bank
├── AI integration
└── Advanced grading

Sprint 6 (Weeks 12-13): Polish & Integration
├── Bug fixes
├── Performance optimization
└── Integration testing

Sprint 7 (Week 14): Deployment & Documentation
├── Final testing
├── Deployment
└── Documentation completion
```

### 7.2 Advantages of 2-Week Sprints in 14-Week Timeline

#### 1. Regular Feedback Loops
- **7 opportunities** to get supervisor feedback
- Each Sprint Review shows working software
- Can adjust course based on feedback
- Reduces risk of building wrong features

#### 2. Early Risk Detection
- Problems discovered within 2 weeks
- Time to address issues before they compound
- Regular retrospectives identify process issues
- 7 chances to improve team performance

#### 3. Incremental Value Delivery
- Working software every 2 weeks
- Can demo progress to supervisor regularly
- Each Sprint adds concrete value
- If project shortened, still have working product

#### 4. Motivation and Momentum
- Short goals keep team motivated
- Sense of accomplishment every 2 weeks
- Prevents burnout from long milestones
- Clear progress markers

#### 5. Flexibility to Adapt
- Can reprioritize after each Sprint
- Respond to supervisor's feedback
- Adjust scope based on velocity
- Handle unexpected requirements

### 7.3 Velocity Planning

#### Initial Estimation
```
Team Capacity per Sprint:
- Sprint length: 2 weeks = 10 working days
- Team members: 4 developers + 1 tech lead = 5 developers
- Hours per day: 6 hours (accounting for classes)
- Scrum ceremonies: -8 hours per Sprint
- Buffer for uncertainties: -20%

Calculation:
Raw capacity: 10 days × 5 devs × 6 hours = 300 hours
Minus ceremonies: 300 - 8 = 292 hours
Minus buffer (20%): 292 × 0.8 = 233.6 hours per Sprint

Story points: ~40-50 points per Sprint (assuming 5-6 hours per point)
```

#### Velocity Tracking
- Measure actual velocity in Sprint 1
- Adjust planning for Sprints 2-7
- Typical pattern:
  - Sprint 1: Lower velocity (learning)
  - Sprints 2-5: Stable/increasing velocity
  - Sprints 6-7: Slightly lower (polish work)

### 7.4 Risk Mitigation with 14-Week Scrum

#### Risk: Feature Creep
**Mitigation**:
- Product Owner controls backlog
- Sprint commitment prevents mid-Sprint additions
- Regular reviews keep scope in check
- Can descope in later Sprints if needed

#### Risk: Technical Debt
**Mitigation**:
- Definition of Done includes code quality
- Technical debt items in backlog
- Reserve capacity for refactoring
- Retrospectives address quality issues

#### Risk: Team Member Unavailability
**Mitigation**:
- Sprint buffer accounts for absences
- Cross-functional skills prevent bottlenecks
- Can adjust Sprint Goal if needed
- Small team can reorganize quickly

#### Risk: Changing Requirements
**Mitigation**:
- Backlog refinement each Sprint
- No changes during Sprint execution
- New requirements go to backlog
- Can reprioritize between Sprints

## 9. Implementation Strategy

  - Q&A session

- **Role Assignment** (2 hours)
  - Product Owner: Confirm responsibilities
  - Scrum Master: Training on facilitation
  - Tech Lead: Technical oversight role
  - Developers: Self-organization principle

- **Values and Principles** (1 hour)
  - Team agreement on Scrum values
  - Working agreement creation
  - Communication protocols

#### Day 3-4: Tools and Environment Setup
- **Development Environment**
  - Git repository setup
  - GitHub Projects or Jira board setup
  - Development environment configuration
  - CI/CD pipeline basics

- **Definition of Done** (2 hours)
  - Draft initial DoD
  - Get team consensus
  - Document and display visibly
  - Review and update each Sprint

- **Communication Tools**
  - Slack/Teams channel
  - Video conferencing setup
  - Document repository (Google Drive/Confluence)

#### Day 5: Initial Backlog Creation
- **Product Backlog Workshop** (4 hours)
  - Product Owner presents vision
  - Team creates initial user stories
  - Rough prioritization
  - High-level estimation

- **Sprint Planning Preparation**
  - Identify Sprint 1 goal
  - Pre-select likely Sprint 1 items
  - Identify dependencies
  - Risk assessment

### 9.2 Sprint Execution Pattern (Sprints 1-6)

#### Sprint Planning (First Day)
**Input**:
- Prioritized Product Backlog
- Team velocity (from previous Sprint)
- Team capacity for upcoming Sprint

**Output**:
- Sprint Goal
- Sprint Backlog
- Task breakdown
- Commitment

**Duration**: 2-3 hours

**Agenda**:
1. Product Owner presents top backlog items (30 min)
2. Team asks clarifying questions (30 min)
3. Team selects items for Sprint (30 min)
4. Team creates task breakdown (60 min)
5. Commitment and Sprint Goal setting (30 min)

#### Daily Scrum (Every Day, Same Time)
**Format**: Stand-up (literally standing)
- **Time**: 15 minutes maximum, same time daily (e.g., 9:00 AM)
- **Location**: Same place (physical or virtual)
- **Structure**: Round-robin, each person answers:
  1. What did I complete yesterday?
  2. What will I work on today?
  3. Do I have any blockers?

**Scrum Master Role**:
- Timekeep (cut off at 15 minutes)
- Note impediments
- Facilitate, don't manage

**Anti-Patterns to Avoid**:
- Status reporting to Scrum Master
- Problem-solving during meeting
- Going over time
- Not everyone participating

#### Mid-Sprint Refinement (Week 2, Day 2)
**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Purpose**: 
- Refine upcoming backlog items
- Add details to high-priority items
- Update estimates
- Clarify requirements

**Participants**: 
- Whole team
- Led by Product Owner

#### Sprint Review (Last Day, Part 1)
**Duration**: 1-2 hours

**Agenda**:
1. Scrum Master sets context (5 min)
2. Product Owner states Sprint Goal and what was planned (5 min)
3. Development Team demos completed work (45-60 min)
4. Product Owner accepts/rejects each item (15 min)
5. Discussion of what to do next (15 min)

**Key Points**:
- Show working software, not PowerPoint
- Get real feedback from Product Owner
- Discuss Product Backlog
- Update completion metrics

**Output**:
- Revised Product Backlog
- Accepted/Rejected work
- Feedback for improvement

#### Sprint Retrospective (Last Day, Part 2)
**Duration**: 1 hour

**Format**: Start-Stop-Continue
1. **Start** - What should we start doing?
2. **Stop** - What should we stop doing?
3. **Continue** - What should we keep doing?

**Agenda**:
1. Set the stage (5 min)
   - Review previous action items
   - Create safe environment
2. Gather data (15 min)
   - Each person adds sticky notes to Start/Stop/Continue
3. Generate insights (20 min)
   - Group similar items
   - Discuss themes
4. Decide what to do (15 min)
   - Vote on top 2-3 improvements
   - Create action items with owners
5. Close retrospective (5 min)
   - Appreciate team members
   - Commit to actions

**Output**:
- 2-3 concrete action items for next Sprint
- Team appreciation and bonding
